1. Introduction to Sheet Metal Design

Sheet metal fabrication is one of the most widely used manufacturing processes in the world. From automotive body panels to HVAC ductwork to aerospace components, bent sheet metal is everywhere.

Understanding how metal behaves during bending is critical to producing accurate flat patterns. The key challenge: when a sheet of metal is bent, the outer surface stretches while the inner surface compresses. The neutral axis — the imaginary plane that neither stretches nor compresses — is what all bend calculations are built around.

This guide covers the essential calculations, material data, and design guidelines that every mechanical engineer, CAD drafter, and fabricator needs.

2. Bend Allowance & Bend Deduction

What is Bend Allowance?

Bend Allowance (BA) is the arc length of the neutral axis between the bend lines. It represents the amount of material consumed by the bend. To create an accurate flat pattern, you add the bend allowance to the two flat leg lengths.

Bend Allowance Formula BA = (π / 180) × A × (R + K × T)

Where:

  • A = Bend angle in degrees
  • R = Inside bend radius
  • K = K-Factor (neutral axis position ratio)
  • T = Material thickness

What is Bend Deduction?

Bend Deduction (BD) is the difference between the total of the two outside setbacks and the bend allowance. It tells you how much to subtract from the total flat length to account for the bend.

Bend Deduction Formula BD = 2 × OSSB − BA

Outside Setback (OSSB)

The distance from the bend tangent point to the apex of the bend on the outside.

Outside Setback Formula OSSB = (R + T) × tan(A / 2)
💡 Pro Tip

For a standard 90° bend: OSSB simply equals R + T, since tan(45°) = 1. This makes quick mental calculations easy on the shop floor.

3. Understanding the K-Factor

The K-Factor is the ratio of the neutral axis location (t) to the material thickness (T). A K-Factor of 0.5 means the neutral axis is exactly at the center of the material. In practice, it's usually less than 0.5 because the inner portion compresses more than the outer stretches.

K-Factor Definition K = t / T   (where t = distance from inside surface to neutral axis)

K-Factor vs. R/T Ratio

R/T Ratio Bend Condition Typical K-Factor Notes
< 1 Tight Bend 0.33 Air bending, high deformation
1 – 2 Standard Bend 0.40 – 0.44 Most common in production
> 2 Loose / Large Radius 0.45 – 0.50 Aesthetic bends, low stress
⚠️ Important

K-Factor values can vary based on bending method (air bending vs. bottoming vs. coining), grain direction, and work hardening. Always validate with test bends when precision is critical.

6. Minimum Bend Radius Guidelines

Bending too tightly can cause cracking on the outer surface. The minimum inside bend radius depends on the material, thickness, and grain direction.

Material Min R (with grain) Min R (against grain)
Mild Steel 0.5T 1.0T
Aluminum 5052-H32 1.0T 1.5T
Stainless Steel 304 0.7T 1.5T
Copper 0T (flat bend ok) 0.5T
Brass 0.5T 1.0T
Titanium 2.0T 3.0T
Spring Steel 2.0T 4.0T
💡 Rule of Thumb

When in doubt, use a minimum inside bend radius equal to the material thickness (R = T). This provides a safe starting point for most materials.

7. Design for Manufacturing (DFM) Tips

Hole & Slot Placement

  • Maintain a minimum distance of 2T + R between the edge of a hole and the bend line
  • If holes must be closer, add relief cuts (notches) along the bend line

Flange Length

  • Minimum flange length should be at least 4T or 6.35mm (0.25"), whichever is greater
  • Short flanges are difficult to hold during bending and may slip

Bend Proximity

  • Keep a minimum of 6T between two parallel bends
  • Closer bends cause material interference and distortion

Grain Direction

  • Bending perpendicular to the grain direction reduces cracking risk
  • If bending in both directions, orient the grain at 45° to the bend lines

Corner Relief

  • Add corner reliefs at least 1T wide and 1T + R deep
  • This prevents tearing at the intersection of two bends
⚠️ Springback

All metals spring back after bending. Stainless steel and high-strength alloys spring back more than mild steel. Overbend by 2-5° to compensate, or use coining for precision bends.

9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using the wrong K-Factor: Don't assume K = 0.5 for all materials. This gives inaccurate flat patterns.
  • Ignoring grain direction: Bending along the grain dramatically increases cracking risk.
  • Placing holes too close to bends: Holes will deform during bending if they're within the deformation zone.
  • Forgetting springback: Always account for elastic recovery, especially with stainless steel.
  • Not specifying inside radius: Fabricators default to their tooling radius if you don't specify R. This changes your flat pattern.
  • Mixing gauge systems: Steel gauge ≠ Aluminum gauge. Always specify actual thickness in mm or inches.
  • Tight bends on galvanized: The zinc coating can crack and flake on bends tighter than 1T radius.

10. Reference Formulas

Bend Allowance BA = (π / 180) × A × (R + K × T)
Bend Deduction BD = 2 × OSSB − BA
Outside Setback OSSB = (R + T) × tan(A / 2)
Flat Pattern Length L_flat = Leg₁ + BA + Leg₂
K-Factor K = t / T   (0 ≤ K ≤ 1)
Neutral Axis Position t = K × T
